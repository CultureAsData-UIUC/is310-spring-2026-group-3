## Digital Objects & Representations  

Some ideas for digital objects and digital representations in our field might include songs, playlists, lyrics, music sheets/compositions, and artists.  

Some digital objects and digital representations in the field of music and social media are songs, playlists, music videos, artist profiles, and engagement metrics.  

- **Songs (MP3, WAV files)** ‚Äì These are direct digital representations of recorded music. They may be digitized from analog recordings or created digitally.  
- **Playlists (Spotify/Apple Music playlists)** ‚Äì These are curated digital collections that show listening behavior, taste, and algorithmic recommendation systems.  
- **Music videos (YouTube, TikTok clips)** ‚Äì These are audiovisual digital objects that shape how music spreads and trends.  

---

## Digitization Processes  

Digital objects in music and social media are created through several processes.  

**Audio Digitization** ‚Äì Analog recordings are converted into digital files using analog-to-digital converters (ADC). Sound waves are sampled and encoded into formats such as MP3 or WAV. This process is similar to scanning a printed poem, but instead of converting text through OCR, it converts sound waves into numerical data.  

**Text Digitization** ‚Äì Lyrics, album booklets, and sheet music are digitized through scanning and OCR. Similar to what was observed in HathiTrust, OCR errors can occur in lyrics databases, especially with stylized fonts or unusual formatting.  

**Social Media Encoding** ‚Äì Videos and audio clips are compressed and formatted for platform compatibility. Algorithms analyze user engagement data to recommend content.  

Compared to HathiTrust, which focuses primarily on text extraction and preservation, music digitization includes audio encoding, visual compression, metadata structuring, and algorithmic distribution. It is both archival and interactive.  

---

## Historical Equivalents  

Historical equivalents to digital music objects include sheet music, vinyl records, cassettes, radio broadcasts, and music magazines.  

- **Sheet music** ‚Äì Before recordings were widely available, sheet music was the primary way music was written and distributed. It is comparable to downloadable PDF scores today.  
- **Vinyl records and cassettes** ‚Äì These were physical storage formats for recorded sound. Streaming services now function as digital equivalents, storing music in remote servers rather than physical discs.  

The major difference is accessibility and scale. Historical formats were physical and localized, while digital music objects are global, searchable, and measurable.  

---

## Born-Digital Materials  

Born-digital materials in music and social media include content that was created digitally and never existed in analog form.  

Examples include:  
- TikTok sounds created directly in the app  
- Instagram live performances  
- SoundCloud-only releases  
- AI-generated music  

Unlike digitized vinyl or scanned sheet music, these materials originate digitally. They are often shorter, optimized for platform algorithms, and dependent on user interaction.  

Digitized objects preserve past material, while born-digital objects define current cultural production. Born-digital music is often shaped by trends, algorithm visibility, and platform-specific features, which distinguishes it from historical digitization efforts focused on preservation.

## Oldest Digital Library/Archive  

The IUMA (Internet Underground Music Archive), which started in 1993, is considered the first free, high-quality music archive on the web.  

**Maintenance and Updates:**  
IUMA provided a way for independent musicians to share music with fans direct from their own home before the major streaming era started; as such, it was the first site of its kind and one of the pioneers of this new way to promote music. It was successful until too many times it was sold and could not maintain itself; after selling it, the majority of its collection has been saved or rescued by the Internet Archive to make sure it would not disappear forever.  

**Metadata and Standards:**  
IUMA and early archives like CANTUS were predominantly run on only basic Descriptive Metadata (artist, title of song, genre). In years past, these systems converted to more complex standards like MARC (used in libraries) and EBUCore (for the specific needs of broadcast and radio).

## Newest Digital Library/Archive  

There are quite a number of Digital Libraries other than That Pitch (which serves both as a Digital Library (Sync Licensing) for using music in Film/Advertising as well as being an incredibly new and specialized platform in 2026).  

**How Library Size Has Evolved:**  
The previous version of IUMA was the simplest type of repository/library; however, the new Digital Libraries have expanded their size and capacity and offer users a superior means of finding content through algorithms.  

**How Metadata Has Evolved:**  
IUMA and earlier versions of Online Databases have depended on traditional tagging methods to organize archives; however, new Digital Libraries have implemented both Technical Metadata (bitrate, file structure) and Administrative Metadata (licensing rights/permissions) to facilitate licensing of materials quickly and simply, as Administrative Metadata will be added automatically to song files.

## Viral Examples  

An example of a viral digital music item is Napster (1999), a peer-to-peer file-sharing service.  

**What Happened:**  
Napster allowed individuals to connect directly to computers where songs were stored on the user‚Äôs hard drive; in essence, each user‚Äôs hard disk becomes part of a gigantic, distributed repository for every song. Napster went viral due to its ability to allow individuals to easily locate and download virtually any song for free.  

**How It Affected the Music Industry:**  
The traditional music business was negatively impacted by what became known as the ‚Äúslashdotted‚Äù effect (also known as ‚Äúslashdot effect‚Äù) by the combination of both an extremely dramatic surge of traffic due to digital distribution and this sudden crash of the traditional music model. As a result, the recording companies began creating legitimate and high-quality digital music repositories, such as the iTunes Store and eventually Spotify, in order to provide the consumer with a legal alternative to the viral activity associated with Napster.

## Free vs. Proprietary Access  

There are two types of archives available to users:  

- Free/public archives that allow listeners to access historical recordings for free, such as the Internet Archive (audio archive), UC Santa Barbara's Cylinder Audio Archive.  
- Proprietary archives which consist of commercially operated proprietary libraries/platforms like Spotify, Apple Music, and Tidal.  

The two archives differ in their methods of access: free/public archives often allow the user to download files (FLAC or MP3) of recordings for personal use and are not restricted in this usage; whereas proprietary libraries operate on a "gatekeeping" basis and charge subscribers for the right to stream music, and the subscriber does not actually own the digital object (for example, if an artist withdraws their library from a proprietary archive because the artist no longer has a licensing agreement with the archive).
