# Instructor Feedback on Initial Dataset Submission

Overall, I can tell you're thinking carefully about some genuinely interesting tensions in this data. Your observation that danceability and energy are Spotify's internal metrics rather than objective measures, and your point about songs with upbeat instrumentals and sad lyrics resisting easy categorization, are both the right kinds of questions to be asking. The instinct to focus on mood as a lens for understanding how meaning gets quantified is interesting and worth developing. That said, there are some significant gaps in the submission that need to be addressed before the project can move forward on solid ground.

**1. The Dataset and the Audit Are Both Unclear**

First, a basic but important clarification: it's not clear from the documentation which of the three Kaggle datasets you actually used to generate your 75-song sample. The submission lists multiple potential datasets but doesn't specify which one you ended up working with. Please make that explicit.

More significantly, like the audit framing in your documentation, the actual auditing work hasn't happened here. You describe your approach as "auditing" but what you've done is clean and sample the data, which is a different thing. Auditing means understanding who created this dataset, for what purpose, and how. Tracing the data back to its origins, trying to verify its accuracy, and identifying what might be missing or distorted. For this particular dataset, that would mean questions like: Who uploaded this to Kaggle and what was their methodology? Was it scraped directly from Spotify? If so, when and how? Can you spot-check specific songs against what Spotify currently shows for them? Are there songs you'd expect to find in a top songs dataset that are missing? I gave specific guidance on how to approach this in my earlier feedback on your topic selection, so please refer back to that document.

The random sampling approach also needs more rationale. Randomly selecting 75 songs from a larger dataset makes it very difficult to identify meaningful patterns, since you have no control over what you end up with or why. What were you hoping to find by sampling randomly rather than purposively? I think part of the issue is that the project's focus hasn't quite crystallized yet, which makes it hard to know what sampling strategy would serve the analysis best.

**2. The Cultural Question Needs More Clarity**

This connects to the broader issue I want to flag: it's not yet clear what cultural phenomenon you're most interested in studying. You mention wanting to understand patterns in popularity and what features contribute to it, but that's a very broad framing. I think there are actually two more interesting and focused questions hiding in your documentation that are worth pulling out and choosing between.

The first is how Spotify turns subjective experience into quantitative metrics. How does a platform decide what "danceability" or "energy" or "valence" means, and how accurate or meaningful are those calculations? This is actually a verifiable question. In the past, students have used Python libraries like `music21` [https://pypi.org/project/music21/](https://pypi.org/project/music21/) to recalculate audio features directly from MIDI files and compare them to Spotify's own scores. You could also take a more personal approach: do you agree with Spotify's danceability assessment for songs you know well? Where does your own judgment diverge from the platform's, and what might that tell you?

The second interesting question is the one you gesture at with the upbeat-but-sad-lyrics example. You might explore how songs can carry seemingly contradictory features that resist single-label categorization. That's a genuinely interesting cultural observation about how mood and meaning in music work. But rather than accepting that tension as a limitation, you could make it the center of the project: what does it mean that a platform has to assign a single "valence" score to a song that contains multitudes?

Clarifying which of these directions interests you most will also help you figure out what data you actually need and how to verify it.

**3. Next Steps**

The plan to use the Spotify Web API to expand the dataset is reasonable in principle, but it needs more specificity. Which songs will you be querying, and why those? What data fields are you planning to retrieve? The API does have rate limits and requires authentication, so building that into your planning early is important. I'd also recommend looking at Spotipy [https://spotipy.readthedocs.io/en/2.26.0/](https://spotipy.readthedocs.io/en/2.26.0/) rather than raw `requests`, it's a Python library built specifically for the Spotify API and will make the process considerably more manageable.

The LLM mood label idea is interesting but needs careful thought before you implement it. What exactly will you be prompting the model with. Is it lyrics, Spotify metrics, both? The prompting strategy will significantly shape the results, and it's worth thinking through what a meaningful assessment would look like versus what would just be noise. One important thing to keep in mind: LLMs will always produce an answer, even when the question is ambiguous or the input is insufficient. That means you'll need a way to evaluate the quality of the labels rather than just accepting the output. Comparing LLM-generated mood labels against Spotify's own metrics could be one approach, but think carefully about what agreement or disagreement between those two would actually tell you.

Overall, there's a genuinely interesting project here around how platforms quantify subjective musical experience, and I think you have good instincts about where the tensions are. The main priorities before the next phase are doing the actual provenance and verification work on the dataset, and narrowing your focus so that the rest of the decisions (what to sample, what to verify, what to prompt) follow naturally from a clear research question. Happy to chat more if you'd like to talk through the direction before you move into scaling.
